Preface

Toys are the forgotten significance. It holds a substantial memorial. It became a mystic item, as the years passed. The dust, a remnant of a different life.

It’s early 2000. We visited grandma. She lived in a flat, build in the post-second war in the Netherlands. I still remember the smell of the staircases in the concrete hallways. It smelled wet, and old, yet musky and nice.

My grandma lived on her own in this little apartment. Her husband - my grandfather and my father's father - died already a long time ago. I have never met him in my life. Her apartment was chic yet old-fashioned decorated. The dark wooden furniture is accentuated with crochet doilies with flowers. Even the television furniture got blessed by a crochet doily, just like her coffee table and the radiator.

You would hear always the following sounds. Ticking from the golden clock, which was held by a white sculpture of a woman. A Christian choir, sung on the television. The tones of a little silver spoon, stirring the cup of coffee of my father. Because he was the only one who drinks his coffee with milk and sugar. And of course; the sound of my grandma talking. The most imperative sound, which made it my grandma’s apartment. Because she could talk a lot.

These memories of my grandma’s apartment are still strong till this day. And I know exactly why. There wasn’t much to play with. So I often found myself being in the moment or daydreaming away. By looking at one of the paintings in the house, for example. In the hallway there was a painting of two women, sitting, almost lying, casually at the beach, looking at the beholder. It looks like a painting made in the 1800s.

‘If I look away, maybe they will move?’ ‘What would they talk about?’ There was also a fake Rembrandt in her house. An image of Titus as a child, the son of Rembrandt. I had a little crush on him. Because he had curls and sweet eyes. Which is still my kinda type. Now it hangs in my room while I type these words.

Then there was this huge closet in another room of my grandma’s apartment. You can easily call it ‘the Closet to the realm of Narnia’. From the Novels of C. S. Lewis. A fantasy world of magic, mythical beasts, and talking animals. The closet was made of a glossy type of wood and felt monstrous. As it curled up at the ceiling. I could eat me up, if my parents and my grandma wouldn’t pay close enough attention.

But suddenly, my dad came up to me with a huge brown suitcase. Filled with little railroads, plastic horses, pigs, cows, trains and cars. It wasn’t much, but it felt as if I made a mystic journey back to the 60s. When my dad was just as old, as I was then; playing on the carpet at my grandma’s place. Hearing the same clock, the Christian choir singing on the radio, surrounded by the same paintings. Maybe his dad was stirring the same cup of coffee with the same little silver spoon as my dad did in my childhood.

Introduction

A toy is a tool that is practiced for life experiences. For kids to understand the world in a broader sense, discover their identity, help with cognition, learn cause and effect, explore relationships, become stronger physically, stimulate creativity, and practice skills needed in adulthood. We take on roles, speak and use body language to represent our character – or our own. We engage in verbal storytelling and take action in the character’s world.

For me – currently – toys serve as bridges and portals. The brown suitcase was the portal, the toys inside became an invisible bridge between me and my father as a child. I never came back to this memory, until now. I got wondering how toys shaped the world of my father. And how that eventually shaped me.

My dad became a toy collector, just like I did. We put them safely in a cabinet, or up high - as pieces of art, or memory of adventures that have been played with the play object itself. Surrounded by fantasy we can roll play or dream about different time eras and dimensions.

I believe that who we are, is all manifestations of what our parents, grandparents, and grand grandparents were as a child. Now we live in an era where we never talk this much about generational trauma. With this research, I plead for the possibility to find answers to what might have been missed, forgotten, or lost of the children in our family line and past. What can we learn from them? What do they have to tell us or to show? What is that spark that toys can spark within us? We are going to explore the relationship between toys, relics, magic, and portals. How do toys form us and what do toys say about us? What makes a toy? What makes a relic? What makes a portal? And why do I think that everyone should be surrounded by toys?

Toys

My relationship with toys has always been something mystic. Dreams, imagination, play, and magic. The combination of the unconscious, conscious, and uncertainty glide us into a world where fantasy becomes reality. Toys, like the play itself, serve multiple purposes in both humans and animals. As we, humans, could kick a ball, a dog would try to chase it. Toys are part of nature. As of today, many items are manufactured to serve as toys, there are items and/or materials for other purposes that can also be used as toys. I mean, what kind of games and play can you come up with, with only sticks and stones?

“Play is the highest development in childhood, for it alone is the free expression of what is in the child’s soul…. children’s play is not a mere sport. It is full of meaning and import.” Fröbel said.

Friedrich Wilhelm August Fröbel was a German educator. He is the founder of the first kindergarten, in 1837 in Bad Blankenburg. It was an institute for children under the age of six, which he occupied with all sorts of self-invented works that he considered instructive. For example, the folding of paper. Children would become acquainted with geometric forms. This kind of play resulted in one of the most known toys to children; the block box. Something what would give a huge influence on some “rebels” in Germany, 100 years later. Which made these wooden toys popular till this day. More on this later. Because let’s go back, way – way – way back. Because where or when did the invention of toys start? How was the relation between children and toys throughout the years? And what made the toys so cultural popular and how?

I will start this with a straight up answer: The origin of toys is found in the doll. Our ancestors began to create dolls to give to the new infant. The earliest documented dolls go back to the ancient civilizations of Egypt, Greece, and Rome. Which would be around 2000BC. The dolls had wigs and movable limbs which were made from stone, clay, sticks, wax and wood. These dolls were blessed with a protective mimic spirit to confuse the evil spirits.

Figure 1 Paddle Doll, ca. 2008-1630 B.C.E. Wood, mud, flax, faience, pigment, (20.3 x 5.2 cm). Like in Ancient Greece and Ancient Rome, children also played with bows and arrows, and yo-yos. When Greek children, especially girls, came of age it was customary for them to sacrifice the toys of their childhood to the gods. On the eve of their wedding, young girls around fourteen would offer their dolls in a temple as a rite of passage into adulthood¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬¬. Here we already see the toy in a ritual manner. I will go more extensive about this topic in Faith and Collecting Repiques. Until the first millennia, toys were to embody spiritualism, superstition and ritual, instead to comfort and joy as we know today

Toys were also important during the medieval era. This was also the time that toys, as term, got documented. With my own hypothesis due to the upcoming of book pressing. Various historians and archaeologists have been able to demonstrate the reflecting matter of the relationships between parents and children in these times A large majority of toys from this era were made from decaying materials like wood, wax, cloth, or even grass. As such most would have decomposed leaving little evidence for the archaeological record, although some toys did survive. The toys of princes and the high nobility were, not surprisingly, of better quality and thus more likely to be well preserved. With the highly craftsmanship, suggesting the owners of these toys were valued individuals. Most toys for princes were also related to the art of warfare to prepare them for what was considered to be their main task in adult life. And sick children could be given toys to pass the time and make them feel better as for Charles VII, future king of France, who fell ill at the age of 2 in 1404 and was given a toy cauldron to play with. Although he was a prince, poorer parents must have behaved similarly with less expensive toys to the extent of their means.

Figure 2 Hortus Deliciarum, Childeren playing with a knight game, end of the 12th century Throughout the first quarter of the 20th Century, most toys were made in Germany. German toys were often made of wood. Hand carving was commonplace and considered manufacturing. Because remember the “rebels” I told you about? Those were the students and graduates of the Bauhaus. Alma Siedhoff-Buscher gave a fresh new breath to the block box that was developed from the plays of Frobel.

Did you know that the Teddy Bear was invented in honor of President Theodore Roosevelt? It all began when Theodore Roosevelt was on a bear hunting trip near Onward, Mississippi on November 14, 1902. Figure 3 Bauhaus Bauspiel: Ein Schiff, Wood, (1923) Alma Siedhoff-Buscher Figure 4 Alma Siedhoff-Buscher As the mass production still had to start (1920’s) many of these toys either had to be skillfully made at home or else they could cost quite a bit of money. In 1900’s, toys for children were more uncommon than we might think. Child labor was more common to children, than it was to play. Some as young as 4, worked in factories, mines, fields, canneries, and tenement sweatshops. This was seen in any country where the industrialization was in its peek. The commonplace was that 18% of all American workers in 1900, were under the age of 16

“All caused by sheer poverty, by the poverty of parents, by the ambition of parents and by the ignorance of parents as well as by the law of market which demands that a product be turned out a the least possible cost to sell at the highest possible price” Jane Addams stated in 1914

Ignorance was seen mostly seen in the religious poor parents. As there was no birth control, many children lived in poor environments, with strict regulations and little needed provisions. Toys were seen as impractical – even idiotic for a child to spend its time with, when it could earn money and pray to god. The children, who were lucky enough to own and play with store brought toys, were children from the wealthy adults. They were carefully made of wood or ivory. Think of rocking horses, a rolling hoop, marbles, bilboquet (a cup and ball game) and phenakistiscope (where an animation will be played when you spin it between your palms). During this same period French bisque dolls with swivel heads and movable arms, legs, and eyes, started to occur in newspapers. This made the start of advertisement to trigger psychological urges to bond a child with their make -believe friends and imaginary siblings. Scan in images of the advertisements at these times Image phenakistiscope Mass production went to full glory after WWII ended, the economy was rising, and the baby boomers populated the world. By the 1950s, manufacturers quickly caught on to the idea that wealthier families would buy an entirely new set of clothing, toys and other gadgets on the way toys were positioned in the marketplace. Like television, which brought in celebrity and character tie-ins to build new toy brands from. The postwar generation was easy seduced, and parents were composed to consume. Gasmasker speelgoed advertentie More varation of advertisement of toys. This was the time that fantasy, joy and comfort were fully capitalized. As magic now also had a place to pay for; in 1955, Walt Disney opened Disneyland Park. Later you had Barbie with more friends and toys with stories lines, that made children almost obliged to follow new fantasy experiences. Fantasy was made and created for you – by capitalism. Important to note is that most – if not all - advertisement until 1960 were promoted with white children. From 1970 advertisement showed the first black children, which became way more frequently leading to the 1980’s. 1970 advertisement In the 1980’s and 1990’s nostalgia from the toys, before big chain stores were dominating the market, were prompted. An no wonder… This is the time when baby boomers were adults and wanted to evoke the spirit they felt when they were a child in the 1940’s and 1950’s. This was also, of course, capitalized. Now every generation after the baby boomers, even me, as Generation Z, will search to the spirit of one self as child found in toy we once played with and probably need to pay for.
